By Bob Bower, Chairman, ACC      Well, once again it‚Äôs time for my annual  article on how to prevent your water pipes from  freezing.   It‚Äôs too early yet to tell if Albuquerque‚Äôs  upcoming winter of  2016-2017 will be unusually  cold or not but I think it‚Äôs always a good time right   now to remind you how to protect your water pipes   from the effects of very cold weather.  Most of the  ‚Äúlocal experts‚Äù on this subject generally agree that  steps need to be taken to protect water pipes if  temperatures are expected to drop below 21 degrees  Fahrenheit.  A broken water pipe inside your home  can cause a lot of extensive and expensive damage!   For you ‚Äúold-time residents‚Äù there is nothing new in  this article so it is really for the benefit of our  newcomers who have joined our community during  the past year or so.    Let‚Äôs start with the outside of your home.   Disconnecting water hoses and protecting outdoor  water faucets are very important.  Water that freezes  in outdoor hoses connected to faucets causes a  buildup of pressure on interior plumbing that can  result in a rupture of the interior pipes if the pressure  is too high.  Damage to these interior pipes near an  outdoor water faucet is often difficult to repair due to  the inaccessibility of these pipes.  Disconnecting  hoses from these outdoor faucets and protecting the  faucets with hard foam insulating cups are good ways  to reduce the chances of damage to these water pipes  that extend through your exterior walls.  These  inexpensive foam cups can be purchased at most  home improvement or hardware stores and are easy  to install.  I‚Äôve also found that it is wise to wrap the  outdoor faucet with some insulation prior to placing  the foam cup over the faucet, particularly if the faucet  is located on the north side of your home or is located  in an area that does not receive any winter sunshine.   Also, if you have any water pipes that are located  outside your home that are exposed to the outside  temperatures, you should consider using insulating  tape or heat tape around these exposed pipes.    Now, let‚Äôs consider the inside of your home.   If Albuquerque experiences a spell of extremely cold  weather where temperatures, particularly at night,  drop to below the 21 degrees Fahrenheit  ‚Äúbenchmark‚Äù, leave the water supply line on at a very  slow drip rate somewhere inside your home.  Water  in ‚Äúslow motion‚Äù is much less likely to freeze than  water that is motionless inside of a pipe.  Normally, a  faucet located far away from where the water enters  your home is the one that you should use for this  purpose.  The cost of water used is insignificant  compared to the cost of repairing damage caused by a  broken water line inside your home. If you are away from  your home during the cold season, make sure that  there is adequate heat inside your home to prevent  internal pipes from freezing.  Even when trying to  save energy costs, don‚Äôt turn the heat too far down  (experts recommend no lower than 55 degrees F)  and don‚Äôt turn the heat off altogether unless you  have all of the water lines properly drained.  Believe  me, I have seen the damage caused by pipes that  have not been properly drained and have  subsequently burst inside the home where the heat  has been turned off!    One final note for those of you who have  swamp coolers.  If you have not already done so, be  sure to drain the water reservoir and disconnect and  drain the water supply line. It doesn‚Äôt take much of a  freeze to damage these cooler components.  Have a  great winter!!
